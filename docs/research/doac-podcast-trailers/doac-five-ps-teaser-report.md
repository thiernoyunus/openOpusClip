# How to make podcast teasers like Diary of a CEO

## TL;DR

- This video is mainly a **story psychology tutorial**, not a detailed Premiere or After Effects tutorial.
- The core framework is the **5 P's of a DOAC teaser**: Prove, Propose, Provide, Promise, Pose.
- It matches what we have been learning: the edit works because the trailer creates curiosity, trust, urgency, and an open loop before the viewer can leave.
- Visually, the tutorial itself is simple: talking head, clean white/purple slides, some DOAC screenshots, big emphasis captions, and occasional black/music pause frames.
- For our future skill, this video should become the **story checklist module**: before captions, B-roll, blur, and transitions, the editor needs to know what each moment is supposed to do.

## The 5 P's Framework

### 1. Prove the Title and Thumbnail

The first job of the teaser is to prove the viewer made the right choice by clicking.

Beginner translation: if the title promises burnout, the opening clip should immediately feel like burnout is real, urgent, and emotional. Do not start with background setup.

Editing action:

- choose a clip that directly connects to the title
- make it emotionally specific
- avoid resolving the answer too early
- treat the teaser like a movie trailer, not a summary

### 2. Propose Why It Matters

The teaser shifts from curiosity to urgency.

Beginner translation: after the viewer thinks "this is interesting," make them feel "I need to know this."

Editing action:

- show the cost of ignoring the topic
- show the reward of understanding it
- use lines that imply consequence
- raise the stakes before explaining the solution

### 3. Provide Social Proof

The viewer needs a reason to trust the guest.

Beginner translation: if the viewer does not already know the guest, use B-roll and text to make the guest feel credible.

Editing action:

- show the guest on stage
- show headlines or awards
- show book covers
- add short credibility captions
- use proof that fits the guest's real level

### 4. Promise Hidden Value

The trailer should hint that the episode contains more than the title promised.

Beginner translation: widen the topic. Make the viewer feel there are layers they have not seen yet.

Editing action:

- hint at a deeper story
- use lines like "what happened next changed everything"
- reveal that the episode contains multiple discoveries
- create a curiosity gap

### 5. Pose a Cliffhanger Question

The ending should leave an open loop.

Beginner translation: do not end by neatly explaining everything. End by making the viewer need the next sentence.

Editing action:

- cut mid-sentence
- fade to black after a shocked reaction
- ask a question with a promised payoff
- end right before the answer

## What This Adds To Our Future Skill

- Add a **5 P's story checklist** before any software steps.
- Teach beginners to label each selected clip by job: prove, propose, provide, promise, or pose.
- Add a drill where the learner takes one podcast transcript and finds one clip for each P.
- Add a review checklist question: "Does this trailer create a psychological journey, or is it just clips stacked together?"

## Skill Exercise Draft

1. Pick a podcast episode title and thumbnail.
2. Write the promise the title makes.
3. Find one clip that proves the promise is real.
4. Find one line that raises the stakes.
5. Add one credibility cue for the guest.
6. Find one line that hints at deeper value.
7. End on a cliffhanger question or unresolved sentence.
8. Only after this works, add captions, B-roll, music, blur, and transitions.
