# i broke down DOAC's INSANE 2026 editing style

## TL;DR

- This is a deeper "how to edit like Diary of a CEO" tutorial built around four pillars: story/trimming, audio and sound effects, guest context, and After Effects motion graphics.
- The biggest lesson is that DOAC intros are not made by effects first. They start with clip selection: hook, value/life lesson, emotional contrast, then a cliffhanger that pushes the viewer into the full episode.
- Captions are treated as a major part of the edit, not an afterthought. The tutorial shows full-screen captions with a blurred/darkened background, bold white text, blue emphasis words, and word-by-word timing synced tightly to the voice.
- Sound design is layered: suspense music, rises, hits, impacts, whooshes, fades between songs, and deliberate music cuts for jokes, bloopers, or cliffhangers.
- The advanced look comes from After Effects polish: AI-generated visuals, keyed/green-screen transitions, paper/photo-frame transitions, vignette, CC Lens distortion, masked blur, turbulence displacement, glow, track mattes, 3D cameras, and animated expression-driven colors.

## Captions, B-Roll, Blur, Transitions, Sound

- **Captions** — DOAC-style captions are selective and cinematic. In the tutorial, the full-screen caption setup uses a blurred/darkened background, large uppercase text, tight line breaks, word-by-word timing, and blue emphasis on the most important words.
- **Caption rhythm** — The text is not treated like normal subtitles. It appears as graphic design: short phrases, bold weight, centered composition, and timing that lands exactly with the speaker.
- **B-roll** — B-roll is used as proof and context. The tutorial shows podcast clips, guest images, AI-generated visuals, screenshots, icons, and stylized object/scene graphics that support the line being spoken.
- **Blur** — Blur is mostly used to separate foreground information from a background plate. For captions, the background becomes softer and darker so the text owns the frame.
- **Transitions** — The transitions are not random preset wipes. The tutorial shows practical transitions built from masks, green-screen/keying, paper/photo-frame movement, bubbles, and animated shapes.
- **Cut scenes** — Strong cuts happen at story turns: when the energy changes, when a new soundbite type begins, when music switches, or when a cliffhanger needs to create tension.
- **Sound design** — Music carries emotional tone. Rises and impacts mark section changes. Whooshes/washes support visual movement. Silence or a hard music cut can make a joke, awkward moment, or cliffhanger hit harder.

## Practical Recipe

1. **Find the story before opening After Effects.** Watch the podcast and mark clips by role: hook, value, emotional contrast, guest context, cliffhanger.
2. **Use AI for first-pass clip hunting.** Export captions, paste the transcript into Claude, and ask it to suggest clips for each story role. Then manually watch those clips because the transcript misses tone, face, and pacing.
3. **Build a rough trailer sequence.** Keep the intro moving from curiosity to value to contrast to unresolved tension.
4. **Add music after the rough story works.** Search for suspense, emotional, or energetic tracks based on the selected clips. Do not force music before the story shape is clear.
5. **Layer sound effects sparingly.** Use rises, impacts, hits, and whooshes to support specific moments instead of covering the whole edit with noise.
6. **Add guest context.** If the audience may not know the guest, add a short voiceover or graphic beat explaining why they matter.
7. **Design captions as graphics.** Use big text, short phrase chunks, strong contrast, and emphasis color only where it helps comprehension.
8. **Polish with custom motion graphics.** Use After Effects for the signature DOAC look: masked transitions, animated objects, depth, glow, camera moves, and tasteful blur/vignette.
