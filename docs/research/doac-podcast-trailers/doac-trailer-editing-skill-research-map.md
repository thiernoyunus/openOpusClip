# DOAC Trailer Editing Skill Research Map

This is not the final skill yet. It is the research map for building a future skill that teaches how to edit Diary of a CEO style podcast trailers.

## Goal

Create a skill that helps a beginner editor learn how these trailers work, then practice recreating the style step by step.

The skill should not only say "add captions" or "use B-roll." It should teach the thinking:

- why a clip is selected
- why the cut happens there
- why the caption looks a certain way
- why the music changes
- why a blur, transition, or motion graphic is used

## Working Theory

DOAC-style trailer editing has two layers:

1. **Story layer** — the actual trailer structure.
2. **Polish layer** — captions, B-roll, transitions, blur, sound design, and motion graphics.

The story layer comes first. If the selected clips are weak, the edit still feels weak even with strong effects.

## Core Story Pattern

Most of the examples point to this structure:

1. **Cold hook** — a surprising, vulnerable, scary, funny, or high-status statement.
2. **Guest or topic context** — why this person or subject matters.
3. **Value / life lesson** — proof that the episode contains useful insight.
4. **Emotional contrast** — switch energy so the trailer does not feel flat.
5. **Stakes** — show what is at risk or why the topic matters now.
6. **Cliffhanger** — cut before the full answer so the viewer wants the episode.

Beginner translation: do not start by asking "what effect should I add?" Start by asking "what feeling should this moment create?"

## 5 P's Story Checklist

This is the clearest beginner checklist from the final tutorial:

1. **Prove** the title and thumbnail promise.
2. **Propose** why the topic matters.
3. **Provide** social proof for the guest or idea.
4. **Promise** hidden value beyond the title.
5. **Pose** a cliffhanger question or open loop.

Beginner translation: every clip in the intro should have a job. If a clip does not prove, raise stakes, build trust, hint at deeper value, or create a cliffhanger, it probably does not belong in the trailer.

## Visual System

What we should turn into lessons:

- **Selective captions** — not every word needs to become a big visual moment.
- **Full-screen captions** — used when the words themselves are the main event.
- **Emphasis words** — important words get color, scale, or stronger timing.
- **Blurred background** — used so captions are easy to read and feel cinematic.
- **B-roll as proof** — B-roll should explain, support, or intensify the line.
- **Cut scenes by meaning** — cuts usually land when the emotion, idea, or music changes.
- **Custom transitions** — masks, paper/card moves, bubbles, green-screen elements, and camera moves feel more premium than basic preset transitions.

## Sound System

What we should turn into lessons:

- **Music chooses the emotion** — suspense, sadness, energy, comedy, or awe.
- **Risers prepare a transition** — they tell the viewer something is about to change.
- **Impacts land the transition** — they make the new moment feel important.
- **Whooshes follow movement** — they support fast visual motion.
- **Music cuts create contrast** — silence or sudden music removal can make a joke, awkward moment, or cliffhanger stronger.
- **Sparse notes deepen emotion** — one piano note can work better than busy music for a sad moment.

## AI Workflow

Useful AI steps from the tutorials:

1. Export the podcast captions or transcript.
2. Ask AI to find possible clips for hook, value, emotion, and cliffhanger.
3. Use AI suggestions as a starting point only.
4. Manually watch the clips because AI cannot fully judge facial expression, tone, pause, or delivery.
5. Use AI to write short guest-context voiceover scripts.
6. Use AI to help with After Effects expressions, but test everything manually.

## Future Skill Modules

### Module 1: Trailer Anatomy

Learn the story structure by labeling reference intros:

- hook
- context
- value
- stakes
- contrast
- cliffhanger

### Module 2: Clip Selection

Practice finding soundbites from a transcript and then checking them by watching the actual footage.

### Module 3: Rough Cut

Build a basic trailer without captions, B-roll, or effects. The goal is to make the story work first.

### Module 4: Music and Sound Design

Add music, risers, hits, impacts, whooshes, and music cuts based on story turns.

### Module 5: Captions

Create DOAC-style captions:

- full-screen caption
- emphasis word
- word-by-word timing
- blurred background
- readable line breaks

### Module 6: B-Roll and Context

Add images, guest context, screenshots, AI visuals, or archive clips only when they support the spoken line.

### Module 7: Transitions

Practice meaning-based transitions:

- hard cut
- blur cut
- paper/card transition
- mask reveal
- green-screen element transition
- motion-graphic object transition

### Module 8: After Effects Polish

Advanced layer:

- masks
- track mattes
- glow
- vignette
- CC Lens / distortion
- turbulence displacement
- 3D camera movement
- expressions

### Module 9: Trailer Review Checklist

Check if the edit works:

- Is the first 5 seconds strong?
- Does every clip have a job?
- Does the trailer give value before asking for more attention?
- Does the music match the emotional arc?
- Are the captions readable without feeling like normal subtitles?
- Are effects supporting the story instead of distracting from it?
- Does the ending create a real reason to watch the full episode?
